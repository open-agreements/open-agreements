# District of Columbia Consumer Privacy Law[^about]

The District of Columbia has no comprehensive consumer-privacy statute; privacy enforcement runs through the Consumer Protection Procedures Act — whose private right of action extends to testers, nonprofits, and public-interest organizations — and a 2020-strengthened breach and data-security law.

## Which privacy laws apply to your business in the District of Columbia? {#which-privacy-laws-apply}

**Short answer.** The District of Columbia has no comprehensive consumer-privacy statute — no general rights of access, deletion, correction, or opt-out, and no controller-processor framework. Two District laws do the work instead. The Consumer Protection Procedures Act (CPPA) makes it a violation for any person to engage in an unfair or deceptive trade practice, whether or not any consumer is in fact misled, deceived, or damaged [^q1-cppa-udap]. The breach-notice duty applies to a person or entity conducting business in the District that owns or licenses covered electronic data and discovers a breach [^q1-breach-notice-scope]. Separate security, vendor-contract, and destruction duties apply under their own conduct triggers in § 28-3852.01 [^q1-security-duty-scope] [^q1-vendor-security] [^q1-destruction], while the definition of person or entity broadly covers private individuals and organizations but excludes the District government itself [^q1-breach-person-entity].

Because there is no omnibus act, District residents have no statutory rights to access, delete, or correct their data, no right to opt out of sale or targeted advertising, and no recognized universal opt-out signal under District law. What fills the gap is the CPPA plus the federal overlay. The CPPA is liberally construed: it must be construed and applied liberally, and it establishes an enforceable right to truthful information from merchants about consumer goods and services purchased, leased, or received in the District [^q1-cppa-liberal]. Misleading privacy settings, undisclosed data sharing, and broken security promises all plead as unfair or deceptive trade practices — which matters here because the CPPA carries a broad private right of action, covered in the consumer-lawsuit section below. The federal layer then applies as it does everywhere: FTC Act § 5 reaches deceptive or unfair privacy practices generally, the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act governs financial institutions, HIPAA governs covered health entities, and COPPA governs services directed to children under 13. This note is written to stay durable: if the Council later enacts an omnibus law, a program built to the CPPA-plus-overlay framework upgrades rather than restarts.

## What must your privacy policy say in the District of Columbia? {#privacy-policy-contents}

**Short answer.** No District statute requires a general consumer privacy policy or fixes what it must contain. The binding rule is that whatever you publish must be true: the CPPA makes it a violation to misrepresent as to a material fact which has a tendency to mislead, or to fail to state a material fact if the failure tends to mislead [^q2-cppa-misrepresent], and FTC Act § 5 separately declares unfair or deceptive acts or practices in commerce unlawful [^q2-ftc-act]. A privacy policy that misstates how you collect, use, share, or secure data is actionable under both.

The CPPA expressly directs courts to give FTC Act § 5 interpretations due consideration and weight: in construing an unfair or deceptive trade practice, courts must give due consideration and weight to how the Federal Trade Commission and the federal courts interpret FTC Act § 5 [^q2-cppa-ftc-alignment]. Federal deception doctrine on privacy promises, omissions, and broken security commitments is therefore an important interpretive guide under District law. Where a sectoral regime applies, it supplies the contents: a HIPAA covered entity must give individuals a notice of the uses and disclosures of their protected health information and of their rights and the entity's duties [^q2-hipaa-notice], a GLBA financial institution must provide a notice that complies with 15 U.S.C. § 6803 before disclosing nonpublic personal information to a nonaffiliated third party [^q2-glba-notice], and COPPA requires covered child-directed operators to provide website notice of child-data collection, use, and disclosure practices [^q2-coppa-notice]. For everyone else, build the policy as best practice — categories of data collected, purposes, third-party sharing, and how users exercise any choices you offer — and then honor it. The stakes of a mismatch are higher in the District than in most sectoral jurisdictions: a misstatement needs no proof that any consumer was actually misled, and it is enforceable not just by the Attorney General but by the CPPA's unusually broad private-plaintiff classes, covered below.

## What must your contracts with vendors say about D.C. residents' data? {#vendor-contracts}

**Short answer.** The District has no omnibus data-processing-agreement statute — no prescribed processing instructions, audit rights, or subprocessor flow-downs. But unlike most sectoral jurisdictions, it does impose one targeted contract duty: a person or entity that discloses a District resident's personal information to a nonaffiliated third-party service provider under a written agreement must require, by that agreement, that the provider implement and maintain reasonable security procedures and practices appropriate to the information and reasonably designed to protect it from unauthorized access, use, modification, and disclosure [^q3-vendor-security].

That clause — added by the 2020 breach-law overhaul — makes a security term in every D.C.-facing vendor agreement a statutory requirement, not just hygiene. It is narrower than a comprehensive-state data processing agreement: it addresses security only, not processing purposes, deletion, or assistance duties. Where a federal regime applies, it adds its own contracting layer: the GLBA Safeguards Rule requires financial institutions to oversee service providers and to require them by contract to implement and maintain appropriate safeguards [^q3-glba-safeguards], and HIPAA requires a business-associate agreement with mandatory data-protection, breach-reporting, and subcontractor terms before sharing protected health information [^q3-hipaa-baa]. The practical drafting move for a vendor template touching D.C. residents is to fold the statutory security clause into the broader best-practice set — processing limited to documented instructions, confidentiality, breach notification back to your business, and return or deletion of data at the end of the engagement — so one rider satisfies the District requirement and the federal overlays at once.

## Does District of Columbia law require you to protect personal data? {#security-safeguards}

**Short answer.** Yes. Since the 2020 breach-law overhaul, any person or entity that owns, licenses, maintains, handles, or otherwise possesses the personal information of a District resident must implement and maintain reasonable security safeguards, including procedures and practices appropriate to the nature of the personal information and to the nature and size of the entity or operation [^q4-safeguards-duty]. This is an affirmative data-security duty that applies before any breach occurs; the quoted duty does not include a separate size threshold, although the reasonableness standard scales to the nature and size of the entity or operation.

The duty extends through the data lifecycle: when destroying records that contain the personal information of a consumer, employee, or former employee — including electronic records and the devices that hold them — the person or entity must take reasonable steps to protect against unauthorized access to or use of that information [^q4-destruction]. The statute scales the standard to the data and the organization rather than defining *reasonable security safeguards* with a control list, so what is reasonable for a small retailer differs from what is reasonable for a data-rich platform. Entities already regulated federally get a deemed-compliance provision: a person or entity subject to and in compliance with the security requirements of the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, HIPAA, or the HITECH Act is deemed to comply with this section [^q4-deemed-compliance]. The teeth come from the enforcement bridge covered below — a violation of the security duty, like a violation of the notice duties, is an unfair or deceptive trade practice under the CPPA.

## When must you notify people of a data breach in the District of Columbia? {#breach-notification}

**Short answer.** Promptly. Any person or entity that conducts business in the District and owns or licenses electronic data including personal information must notify every District resident whose personal information was included in a breach, in the most expedient time possible and without unreasonable delay [^q5-resident-notice]. If the breach affects 50 or more District residents, written notice must also go promptly to the Office of the Attorney General — no later than when residents are notified [^q5-ag-notice]. And when a breach requires notification under § 28-3852(a) or (b) and includes or is reasonably believed to include Social Security or taxpayer identification numbers, the entity must offer identity-theft protection services at no cost for at least 18 months to each District resident whose Social Security number or taxpayer identification number was released [^q5-credit-monitoring].

The trigger is broad. A *breach of the security of the system* means the unauthorized acquisition of computerized or other electronic data — or any equipment or device storing such data — that compromises the security, confidentiality, or integrity of personal information [^q5-breach-def]. *Personal information* covers far more than Social Security numbers: a name or other identifier combined with a government ID number, a financial-account or card number with its access code, medical information, genetic information, health-insurance information, or biometric data [^q5-personal-info] — and, standing alone, a username or e-mail address combined with a password or other means of authentication that permits access to the person's e-mail account [^q5-credentials]. Two exclusions matter operationally. Properly encrypted or redacted data is outside the definition unless the protection itself was compromised. And the risk-of-harm exit is not unilateral: an acquisition falls outside the definition only if the entity reasonably determines, after a reasonable investigation *and consultation with the Office of the Attorney General and federal law enforcement*, that harm to the individual is unlikely [^q5-harm-exclusion]. The Attorney General notice must include eleven enumerated items, from the cause of the breach to a sample of the resident notice [^q5-ag-notice]. Entities that give notice under the GLBA or HIPAA/HITECH breach-notification regimes can satisfy the resident-notice duty that way, but that does not eliminate any Attorney General notice required by § 28-3852(b-1) [^q5-deemed-compliance].

## Can a consumer sue your business in the District of Columbia over privacy? {#consumer-lawsuit}

**Short answer.** Yes — and not only consumers. The CPPA grants a private right of action to a consumer; to an individual tester when the trade practice involves consumer goods or services purchased or received to test qualities for personal, household, or family purposes; to a nonprofit organization on behalf of itself, members, or the general public; and to a public interest organization on behalf of a consumer or class that could sue as consumers, subject to the sufficient-nexus dismissal rule [^q6-standing]. A prevailing plaintiff may recover treble damages or $1,500 per violation, whichever is greater, plus punitive damages, attorney's fees, and an injunction [^q6-remedies]. Data-breach and data-security failures are privately actionable too: a violation of the breach subchapter is an unfair or deceptive trade practice [^q6-bridge], enumerated in the CPPA's list of violations [^q6-kk]. For § 28-3904(kk), the damages remedy is actual damages rather than treble or $1,500-per-violation statutory damages, and actual damages exclude dignitary damages; fees and injunctive relief remain separately listed [^q6-remedies].

The standing grants do real work, but they carry conditions. Testers must fit the consumer-goods-or-services testing language, nonprofits must stay within the nonprofit route, and public-interest organizations can sue only where the represented consumer or class could sue as consumers and the organization has a sufficient nexus to the interests represented [^q6-standing]. Public-interest and general-public claims also face a stay rule: an overlapping Attorney General action under § 28-3909 stays those private claims until the Attorney General action is resolved, and public-interest or general-public plaintiffs must notify OAG within 10 days after filing [^q6-stay]. CPPA claims belong in D.C. Superior Court [^q6-remedies], where the statutory grants, rather than the federal injury-in-fact doctrine, frame who can sue. The two-track damages design is a deliberate split: privacy *deception* claims (a privacy policy or setting that misrepresents practices, under § 28-3904(e) or (f)) carry the full treble-or-$1,500-per-violation remedy, while *breach* claims routed through § 28-3904(kk) have an actual-damages remedy. Even on the breach track, fee-shifting and injunctive relief remain available [^q6-remedies], so the carve-out narrows the damages exposure without closing the courthouse door.

## Who enforces privacy law in the District of Columbia? {#enforcement}

**Short answer.** There is no privacy-specific regulator; public enforcement runs through the CPPA and the breach subchapter. A consumer can begin an administrative case by filing a complaint with the Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection describing the trade practice [^q7-complaint], and in that administrative path the Attorney General for the District of Columbia represents the Department in court proceedings under the Act [^q7-ag-role]. For data breaches and security failures, the breach subchapter's rights and remedies are cumulative with every other remedy available under law [^q7-cumulative]. Cumulative remedies do not erase the CPPA stay and notice rules: an overlapping Attorney General action under § 28-3909 stays public-interest-organization or general-public private claims until the Attorney General action is resolved, and those plaintiffs must notify OAG within 10 days after filing [^q7-stay].
